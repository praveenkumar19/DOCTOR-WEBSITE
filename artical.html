Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

The most common type of heart disease and leading cause of heart attacks

What is Coronary Artery Disease?

Coronary artery disease (CAD) is a condition where the major blood vessels that supply your heart with blood, oxygen, and nutrients become damaged or diseased. Cholesterol-containing deposits (plaque) build up in your arteries, causing them to narrow and harden – a process called atherosclerosis.

This restricts blood flow to your heart muscle. When blood flow is completely blocked, it causes a heart attack. CAD is the most common type of heart disease and the leading cause of death worldwide.

Risk Factors

High Blood Pressure

Damages arteries and accelerates plaque buildup

High Cholesterol

Directly contributes to plaque formation

Diabetes

Increases risk by 2-4 times

Smoking

Damages artery walls and reduces oxygen

Obesity

Puts extra strain on your heart

Sedentary Lifestyle

Weakens heart muscle

Symptoms

Common Symptoms

  • Chest pain (angina): Pressure, tightness, or squeezing in the chest
  • Shortness of breath: Especially during physical activity
  • Fatigue: Extreme tiredness, especially in women
  • Pain spreading: To shoulders, arms, neck, jaw, or back
  • Heart attack: Chest pain with nausea, cold sweat, dizziness

Emergency Warning Signs

Call emergency immediately if you experience:

  • Chest discomfort lasting more than a few minutes
  • Pain spreading to shoulders, neck, or arms
  • Shortness of breath with or without chest pain
  • Cold sweat, nausea, or lightheadedness

Diagnosis

1. Physical Exam & History

Doctor checks blood pressure, listens to heart, and reviews symptoms and risk factors

2. Blood Tests

Check cholesterol levels, blood sugar, and proteins that indicate heart damage

3. ECG (EKG)

Records electrical signals to detect heart damage or irregular rhythms

4. Echocardiogram

Ultrasound to see heart structure and function

5. Stress Test

Monitors heart during exercise to detect reduced blood flow

6. Coronary Angiography

X-ray with dye to see blockages in arteries

Treatment Options

Lifestyle Changes

  • Heart-healthy diet (low in saturated fats, high in fiber)
  • Regular exercise (30 minutes daily)
  • Weight management
  • Stress reduction techniques
  • Quit smoking and limit alcohol

Medications

  • Statins: Lower cholesterol
  • Beta-blockers: Reduce heart workload
  • ACE inhibitors: Lower blood pressure
  • Antiplatelet drugs: Prevent blood clots
  • Nitroglycerin: Relieves chest pain

Procedures

  • Angioplasty & Stenting: Opens blocked arteries
  • Coronary Artery Bypass Surgery: Creates new routes for blood flow
  • Atherectomy: Removes plaque from arteries

Frequently Asked Questions

Can coronary artery disease be reversed?
While you can't completely reverse CAD, aggressive lifestyle changes and medications can slow progression, reduce symptoms, and in some cases, partially reverse plaque buildup.
What is the life expectancy with CAD?
With proper treatment and lifestyle changes, many people with CAD live long, active lives. The key is early detection and consistent management.
When should I see a cardiologist?
If you have risk factors (high BP, cholesterol, diabetes) or symptoms like chest pain, shortness of breath, or fatigue, consult a cardiologist immediately.

What is Cardiomyopathy?

Cardiomyopathy is a group of diseases that affect the heart muscle. It makes it harder for your heart to pump blood to the rest of your body, which can lead to heart failure. There are several types of cardiomyopathy, each with different causes and treatments.

Types of Cardiomyopathy

Dilated Cardiomyopathy

Heart chambers enlarge and weaken, making it hard to pump blood efficiently. Most common type, often affects middle-aged adults.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

Heart muscle thickens, making it harder for blood to leave the heart. Often genetic and can affect young people, including athletes.

Restrictive Cardiomyopathy

Heart muscle becomes stiff and rigid, preventing proper filling between beats. Least common type, often caused by other diseases.

Arrhythmogenic Cardiomyopathy

Heart muscle replaced with fatty tissue, leading to irregular heartbeats. Rare but serious, often affects young adults.

Genetic Connection

Many cardiomyopathies are inherited. If you have a family history of cardiomyopathy or sudden cardiac death, genetic testing and screening of family members is recommended.

Symptoms

  • Shortness of breath with exertion or at rest
  • Swelling in legs, ankles, and feet
  • Bloating in abdomen due to fluid buildup
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Irregular heartbeats (palpitations)
  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Chest pain, especially after heavy meals or exercise

Diagnosis

Echocardiogram

Ultrasound to see heart structure and function

Cardiac MRI

Detailed images of heart muscle tissue

ECG/EKG

Records electrical activity and rhythm

Identifies inherited forms

Small tissue sample in rare cases

Treatment Options

Medications

  • Beta-blockers: Slow heart rate and reduce workload
  • ACE inhibitors: Lower blood pressure
  • Diuretics: Remove excess fluid
  • Anticoagulants: Prevent blood clots
  • Antiarrhythmics: Control irregular rhythms

Devices & Procedures

  • Pacemaker: Regulates slow heart rate
  • ICD: Prevents sudden cardiac death
  • Septal ablation: Reduces thickened muscle
  • Heart transplant: For end-stage disease

Lifestyle Management

  • Low-sodium diet
  • Fluid restriction
  • Avoid alcohol and stimulants
  • Moderate exercise as approved
  • Daily weight monitoring

FAQs About Cardiomyopathy

Can I exercise with cardiomyopathy?
Yes, but with restrictions. Moderate, low-impact exercise is often beneficial, but competitive sports and heavy lifting may be prohibited. Always consult your cardiologist first.
Is cardiomyopathy curable?
While most types aren't curable, they are treatable. Many patients live normal lives with proper management. In some cases, heart transplant may be needed.
Should my family be tested?
If you have genetic cardiomyopathy, first-degree relatives (parents, siblings, children) should be screened. Genetic counseling is recommended.

What is Congenital Heart Disease?

Congenital heart disease (CHD) refers to one or more problems with the heart's structure that are present from birth. These defects occur while the baby is developing in the womb and affect how blood flows through the heart. CHD is the most common type of birth defect.

Common Types

Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)

Hole in the wall between the lower chambers. Most common CHD.

Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)

Hole in the wall between the upper chambers.

Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)

Connection between aorta and pulmonary artery fails to close.

Tetralogy of Fallot

Combination of four defects causing low oxygen levels.

Coarctation of Aorta

Narrowing of the main artery (aorta).

Transposition of Great Arteries

Pulmonary artery and aorta are switched.

What is Pericarditis?

Pericarditis is inflammation of the pericardium, the thin, sac-like tissue that surrounds your heart. This condition can cause sharp chest pain and other symptoms. It can be acute (sudden and short-term) or chronic (long-lasting).
